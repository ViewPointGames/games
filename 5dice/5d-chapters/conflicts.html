Conflicts

Conflicts drive a story. Conflict embodies the core concept of pitting multiple things against each other. In Mortal Engines: XXXXXX, a conflict describes actions taken by multiple characters that have mutually exclusive goals.

Conflict Types

Two types of conflicts arise throughout the game: Oppositions and Encounters.
Oppositions are quickly resolved by all involved characters performing their separate Skill Checks based on the action they want to take. The result dictates the outcome based on the interpretation table:

Interpreting the result

Result Outcome
3 or less Critical Fail
4 to 6 Simple Fail
7 to 9 Complicated Success
10 to 14 Simple Success
15+ Critical Success

Looking up the Result in the table above allows for interpretation of the Outcome. In this step, the Player and Narrator have to cooperate to tell a fitting and narratively satisfying Outcome for this task.

Example

Barbara rolled an 11 when trying to lockpick an old lock, resulting in a Simple Success. Barbara is able to pick the lock easily without any guard nearby being alerted.

Example

Grimsby achieved a 7 when trying to jump up the ledge. This barely reaches the a Complicated Success. The Player offers to take an Injury as Complication from misjudging the jump. The Glossary > Narrator describes how Grimsby takes a running start, jumps off and is able to reach the ledges height, but he jumped too early and now hangs on the edge of the ledge with his paws. The hard jolt on his arms strains the muscles and bones, and Grimsby has received a Strain through this Glossary > Action.

Encounter Types

Encounters are an umbrella term for multiple types of conflicts that usually are not resolved completely within a few seconds.
Encounters have three types:

  1. Environmental Challenge
  2. Social Parley
  3. Skirmish

Environmental Challenge

Environmental Challenges are most commonly seen on journeys, but they may also be presented within a safe location or indoors. Environmental Challenges usually consist of an obstacle that has to be overcome, but can also be a point of interest that needs to be investigated, or a room with a puzzle that needs to be solved in order to allow the characters to progress.
These challenges are mostly defined through the specific challenge they present the characters, but they all have the following things in common:

  1. Goal: Every challenge comes with a goal that can be reached by performing skill checks and applying clever solutions to problems within the challenge.
  2. Problems: Every challenge also needs obstacles that make it harder for the characters to reach the goal, which the players can solve creatively within the narrators discretion. Failing to overcome these problems may have consequences for the characters.
  3. Complications: Some challenges may apply special complications that make the situation less clear and provide unique threats to circumvent.
Example

The characters have to balance over a fallen tree in oder to cross a river that could harm them severely. They are followed by a group of hungry wolfes, who are not yet in line of sight but have followed their scents.
The goal in this case is to reach the other side, in order to flee from the wolfes. The problem is the river, with an obvious possible solution being presented. The consequences of failing to balance the tree can range from simply getting wet to even be sucked under the surface from the water stream and be injured severely. The wolfes act as a complication in the form of a timer, where too much stalling would result in a combat encounter rather than a successful escape.

Example

A character discoveres a corpse hidden under a pile of books inside a library, with an ornate wooden crate next to the scene.
The goal is defined as finding out what happened. The problem is not having enough information to deduce anything useful, with the consequence of failure being deducting the wrong results. Although not presented in this case, a potential obstacle could be a police force already investigating the scene and hiding away valuable evidence.

Social Parley

When characters meet another, a social parley can begin. Here, similar to environmental challenges, characters are tasked to reach a goal that lies behind a problem, with potential complications in the mix. These elements are however more of metaphysical nature, and so most parleys are held off using mental skills.

Example

One character arrives at a market to buy a certain rare item.
The goal is to obtain the item and return to the party, with the problem being a haggle that is about to ensue.
A complication may arise in the form of another customer, who also shows interest in the same rare item.

Skirmish

Skirmishes are defined by two or more factions trying to defeat each other through violent means. A faction is defined as one or more characters with the same goal within this encounter. Should the goal of a character shift within the encounter, that character remains within the faction for the rest of the encounter.
At the start of Skirmishes, the narrator defines the factions based on their common goals. Skirmishes change the game into a turn-based setup, where all factions take turns performing actions to achieve their own goals.

Tip

To quicken this process, in most circumstances preplanned encounters can have their factions predefined, and most parties can be summed as one faction.

In general, the faction that opens the skirmish by performing the first attack begins the battle. Then, each faction rolls a D20 to determine the order of turns. All factions are now performing their actions and reactions in that incremental order, with the first turn done by the faction that opened the battle. Should a character gain a surprise round on one or more other characters, those surprised can not react to the surprising characters actions.

Example

The party of 4 players is sent to obtain a high ranking artifact from a nearby small military convoy. One of the members notices the convoy being ambushed by a group of bandits. They manage to sneak up, just as the bandits exit their hiding spots to surround the convoy.
Then, the party launches a surprise attack against the bandits. A skirmish starts, and the factions are declared: The bandits all have the common goal to rob the convoy of all their valuables, putting them all into one faction. The players are all trying to somehow obtain the artifact, placing them in another faction. The convoy, who tries to fend off both factions, is the third faction.
All factions now roll a D20 to determine the turn order. The player faction, who started the conflict, acts first. Their roll is a 7. The convoy rolls a 15, and the bandits roll a 1. Going in incremental looping order, after the players are done the convoy acts, and then the bandits get their turn.

Skirmish Combat

Faction turns

All members of a faction share a simultaneously happening turn, during which each character gets 2 actions and 2 reactions. Each action and each reaction takes about 2 to 3 seconds to perform.
Actions are spent during a characters factions own turn, while reactions are spent outside of that turn.

Within a Skirmish, characters may choose to perform different actions based on their position, their equipment, or their skills.
While equipment does offer more equipment specific actions, every character has access to a list of core actions. These core actions include:

  • Move (RFX): This action lets the character move a moderate distance, adding to or subtracting from this distance based on the characters RFX skill.
  • Attack with weapon: This action uses a currently held weapon to injure to a target within weapon reach. The action skill depends on the weapon the character uses.
  • Attack without weapon (STR): This action uses a characters unarmed body to strike a target within melee reach.
  • Interact: This action is a catch-all action for any activity not included in the standard list that can be performed without any special equipment. The action skill depends on the specific action the character performs.
  • Support: This action lets the character notice another character attempting a difficult task, and offering their help.
  • Dodge (RFX) (Reaction): This reaction lets the character dodge an incoming attack to take no damage. Worn equipment may reduce effectivity of this action.
  • Block (STR) (Reaction): This reaction lets the character block an incoming attack to take less damage. Worn equipment may increase effectivity of this action.
  • Interact (Reaction): This reaction is a catch-all reaction for any activity not included in the standard list that can be performed without any special equipment. The reaction skill depends on the specific reaction the character performs.
  • Support (Reaction): This reaction lets the character notice another character attempting a difficult task, and offering their help.
    Held items can also be used to take combat actions, although these actions are not listed here but explained in the items section.

Action "Move (RFX)":

The Move action is the most basic action a character can take. They can use this to reposition themself in a battle, hide behind cover, sneak up on someone, or simply evade an area. When a character takes the move action, they are able to relocate themselves up to a certain distance for this one action. This distance is known as their movement speed, which depends on the characters abilities, equipment and lineage.
By default, characters are able to move in 4 cardinal directions, they can jump down ledges, and climb climbable surfaces or objects to move up or down in the environment. The characters feats, talents and lineage may influence these basic movement options.
Typically, movement is calculated in metric units using meters as the base line.

Action "Attack":

For the purpose of simplicity, this paragraph explains the rules for attacking both with and without a weapon.

When a character makes an attack against a target within (weapon) reach, they choose whether to attack cautiously or recklessly. A cautious attack reduces the resulting injury severity while granting a surge, and a reckless attack increases the injury severity while granting a stall.
The attacker also chooses the hit zone they want to attack on the target. The target may perform a reaction in response to the attack, if they are able and allowed to. If the target does not react, the narrator sets the difficulty for the action. If the target does react, the resulting opposition gets resolved like any other opposition, with each character rolling their respective skill dice and adding static modifiers, then comparing the result using the defender as the difficulty level:

  • If the attacker fails the attack, the attack misses, or the target dodges or blocks successfully, depending on the targets reaction.
  • If the attacker rolls the targets number, the attacker deals one strain.
  • If the attacker rolls between 1 and 4 higher than the target, the attacker deals one wound.
  • If the attacker rolls more than 4 higher than the target, the attacker deals one trauma.

Hit Zones
Every character has 3 defined hit zones: The bulk, the brim, and the ace.
When a character tries to physically attack another character, the attacker declares the hit zone they are going for. Depending on the targets body, each hit zone can have one or more areas.

  • The bulk is the center of mass on a target. This zone is the easiest to hit, but it does not offer any further benefits.
  • The ace is the targets weak point. With humans, this usually is the head. Attempting to hit this zone increases increases the hit threshold by 2. Hitting this zone increases the damage severity by one tier.
  • The brim consists of any other limbs that are attached to the bulk or any other limbs. Limbs can be targeted to disable them until they are treated. Attempting to hit this zone increases the hit threshold by 1. Hitting a limb disables that limb and any other limbs that are attached to the bulk through that limb.

Action "Interact":

This action defines any action that can not be placed under any other directly defined action. The boundaries and limitations for the characters actions underly the narrators discretion. As guidelines, here are helpful tips to make this process easier for both parties:

  • One turn takes about 5 seconds in total based on the number of default actions and reactions each character is able to perform. Therefore, if a character wishes to do something that takes longer than that, it would likely take more than one round to complete.
  • Depending on the characters equipment, positioning, and surroundings, the player may have unique ideas for how to interact with their environment.
  • A character may use an interact action to generate progress on a currently active progress clock, which segments do not take longer than 2 actions each.
  • The difficulty of this action lies at the narrators discretion, like a common skill check.

Reaction "Support":

This action represents the character recognizing another character trying to accomplish a difficult goal and offering their help. The character may perform a temporary Approach Skill Check at the same time as the character they try to help at the same difficulty using the Synergy skill and the most appropriate skill for this check. When doing this, their roll acts as a Surge momentum for the character they are supporting.

Reaction "Dodge":

This reaction represents a character evading a direct incoming attack to prevent damage from being taken. As a reaction, the character may only perform this outside their own turn. Dodging uses the RFX skill. This reaction is resolved like a normal Skill Check with no additional modifier:

  • On a critical success, the character manages to evade the incoming attack so much that it decreases the resulting injury by 2 tiers.
  • On a simple success, the character manages to evade the incoming attack so much that it decreases the resulting injury by 1 tier.
  • On a complicated success, the player may choose to reduce the incoming injury by 1 tier at the cost of gaining Stall momentum on their next action or any similar negative draw.
  • On a simple fail, the character does not manage to evade the incoming attack, and gains Stall momentum on their next action or any similar negative draw.
  • On a critical fail, the character evades so badly that they directly either increase this incoming injury by 1 tier or they gain a second Strain tier injury.

Reaction "Block":

This reaction represents a character holding an attack with some object to prevent damage from being taken. As a reaction, the character may only perform this outside their own turn. Blocking uses the STR skill. This reaction is resolved like a normal Skill Check with no additional modifier:

  • On a critical success, the character manages to block the incoming attack so much that it decreases the resulting injury by 2 tiers
  • On a simple success, the character manages to block the incoming attack so much that it decreases the resulting injury by 1 tier.
  • On a complicated success, the player may choose to reduce the incoming injury by 1 tier at the cost of gaining Stall momentum on their next action or any similar negative draw.
  • On a simple fail, the character does not manage to block the incoming attack, and gains Stall momentum on their next action or any similar negative draw.
  • On a critical fail, the character blocks so badly that they directly either increase this incoming injury by 1 tier or they gain a second Strain tier injury.

Reaction "Support":

This reaction represents the character recognizing another character trying to accomplish a difficult goal and offering their help. As a reaction, the character may perform a temporary Approach Skill Check on another factions turn at the same difficulty using the Synergy skill and the most appropriate skill for this check. When doing this, their roll acts as a Surge momentum for the character they are supporting.

Resolutions

Most encounters end once one faction manage to complete their goal. This could be killing all the other characters, or stealing some item or money, climbing a facade, solve a puzzle, or even delivering a simple message.
At this point, it is up to both the narrator and the situation to resolve the conflict using what happened during the encounter. For this, the players are encouraged to provide ideas or even initiate resolutions as roleplay (see What is roleplaying?).

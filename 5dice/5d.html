5D

Introduction and game preparation

This roleplaying game is a Setting-agnostic cooperative storytelling System that is played by at least 2 people. The game can be adapted to fit a multitude of different Settings. In a game of 5D, Players come together to collectively tell a Story within an agreed upon Setting using Roleplaying as the main driver behind Character choices, while Outcomes of Actions with uncertain Results are determined mostly through Dice Rolls. One Player known as the Narrator describes the World of the Story and important elements in the surroundings of the Player Characters, which are controlled by the other Players. Each Player may take control over one or more Characters, with any left over Characters being taken over by the Narrator.

Required Materials

Every Player needs to have access to two full sets of standard polymetric Dice, including the D4, D6, D8, D10, D12, D20 and D%). Furthermore, all Players should have read and understood the rules that are relevant to their Role, including any Setting specific rules which can alter the game.

Preparation

Setting specific preparation

Every Setting follows the core rules laid out in this document, but may add their specific Mechanics and Subsystems to enhance or alternate the experience. In case a Setting requires more preparation than is listed here, the Setting is listing the preparation steps needed separately in its own section.

After all Players can agree on a specific type of Story, they collectively decide who should be the Narrator, the rest will continue asCharacters. Every Player may take control over one or more Characters within the story to dictate those Characters Actions. These picked Characters are called Player Characters. The Narrator is a special Role among the Players. They are responsible for describing the World the Story takes place in and the Actions performed by the Player Characters and how they affect the environment.
In principal, every Player should begin the game with a certain goal in mind, something they wish to achieve during this game. This goal helps inform the narrator on where to take the Story.

Once the bearer of the Narrator Role has been decided, the Narrator can begin the game preparation.
Preparing a game of 5D is much like planning out certain events of a Story. A Story is typically structured into one or more Arcs, each divided into Chapters, which in turn can be divided into one or more Scenes.

Picking a Chapter goal

When starting preparation of a new game of 5D, the narrator need only to pick a Chapter goal, as this is usually the amount of Story that a game can provide within a single session. This Chapter goal is informed by the overarching currently active Narrative and the major plot points who are driving the Story forward. The Narrator is also advised to take the Players goals into consideration at this point, to increase Player engagement. The goal can be newly created or a preexisting one from a previous adventure.

Assessing the situation

After the goal is defined, next up the Narrator should assess the current situation of the main party, where they are located and what they are planning to do. This is the point where game preparation can differ wildly depending on whether the Players are starting a new Story or continuing a previous adventure.
If the Players continue a previous adventure, the narrator is advised to revisit the scenes of the last game, to inform themselves of where the Players left off. This takes off a lot of the prep work on this step required, as it already establishes who the Characters are, how they met and what they each want to accomplish in the story at this point. Should the Players start a new Story, they have to collectively come up with all the answers to this from scratch. It is helpful to answer these questions in order to begin a new Story:

  • Who is each character?
  • What do they have in common?
  • What differentiates them?
  • How did they first meet?

Providing options

The Narrator may want to add a few different options in order to achieve the goal the Players have set out to accomplish, which ideally cover different play styles to ensure Players have different viable choices to pick their favorite path from. Finally, to map out the beats of the Story, the Narrator should prepare a few core scenes that will be key in achieving the goal through one or more options.

Mapping out the beginning

Finally, when the goal is known and the path there roughly mapped out, the Narrator can connect back with the Players and figure out an opening Scene.

Playing the game

Structure

By the nature of the game, 5D is best used for telling a Story over one or more sessions.
Like any Story, its structure can be broken down into Arcs, each being made up of Chapters, which in turn can be broken down even further into Scenes.
All of these Story chunks can be as large or small as the Narrator deems fit. They can be a tiny throw-away line skipping a whole Chapter, or they could use a few hours on a single Scene.

Skill Checks

Skill Checks

Skill Checks are a tool to determine the Outcome of any interaction with not predetermined Results. They can be used whenever a Character attempts something and the Outcome is not fully known.
A Skill Check always follows a simple structure: First, the Narrator determines the Skill to be used and the Difficulty of the task. Then, the Player rolls the required Dice and sums up the Result, using any applicable Modifiers. Finally, the Narrator and the Player work together to interpret the Result into an Outcome that fits within the Narrative of the game.

Determining the Difficulty

Value Name
0 Trivial
1 Uncommon
2 Challenging
3 Extreme
4 Impossible
5 Inconceivable

In order for a Character to see how well they do on a given Skill Check, the Narrator has to set the Difficulty. This Difficulty is a numeric value ranging from 0 to 5 that increases linearly with rising difficulty of the task. The name of that numerical value indicates the intended Difficulty of that Skill Check for an average Character of that Lineage within the Setting.

Example

Barbara wants to pick a lock. Because the lock is quite old and posesses a rather simple mechanism, the Narrator sets this Skill Check as an Uncommon Grace task.

Example

Grimsby is trying to jump up a tall ledge. The narrator sets the Skill Check as a Challenging Might task. Since Grimsby is a kangaroo and not a human, he gets to reduce the Difficulty by 1 tier for jumping related actions as part of his Lineage. Thus, Grimsby has to succeed at an Uncommon task rather than a Challenging one to succeed in this Action.

Assembling the DicePool

Once the Narrator announces the Skill used for the task, the Player can pick up the required dice to roll in the check depending on the Characters training level in that Skill:

Skill Points Dice
-1 (Inept) 2D4
0 (Novice) 2D6
1 (Journeyman) 2D8
2 (Expert) 2D10
3 (Master) 2D12
Example

Barbara tries to pick the lock. Her Grace is at +1, giving her access to a 2D8 Dice Pool.

Example

Grimsby has 2 Skill Points in Might, allowing him to roll 2D10 on his attempt to jump up the ledge.

Difficulty and Skills

Depending on the task at hand, the Difficulty can vary. Oftentimes, the task does not exactly match the Characters Skill level perfectly, which offsets the two values.
Depending on the constellation of these two variable tiers, different Skill Check Modifiers apply automatically:

Character Skill - Task Difficulty Modification
-2 or less Static -5
-1 Stall momentum
0 Normal roll
1 Surge momentum
2 or more Static +2 or automatic simple success

Apart from outright failing or succeeding a task, two more types of Modifiers exist that can change the Outcome of a Skill Check: Momentum and Static Modifiers.

Momentum
Rolling with Momentum means rolling the same Skill Check twice, and taking the higher or lower Result, depending on whether the Character is Surging (higher result) or Stalling (lower result).
A Character is Surging once at least one active effect grants them Surge, but no Stalling effect is applied.
Likewise, a Character is Stalling once at least one active effect applies Stall to them, but no Surging effect is granted.
Should a Character have any combination of at least one Surging and at least one Stalling effect applied to them, they cancel each other out and the Character does not gain any of these effects.

Static Modifiers
Static Modifiers are a way to adjust the Result of a roll and adding a small number to it, be it positive or negative.
After the Dice Roll (including a potential Momentum roll) is made, the player adds all applicable Static Modifiers to the rolled number, forming a final Result.

Example

Barbara wishes to pick a lock with a simple mechanism. The Narrator decided that this is an uncommon task. Barbaras Skill Level is 1, which matches the task Difficulty. She thus has to roll the check once.

Example

Barbara tries to pick an old lock using her lockpicking equipment. This picklock grants her a simple +1 on lockpicking Actions. Barbara uses the 2D8 from her Skill and rolls a 10. The picklocks Static Modifier boosts her result to 11.

Example

Grimsby the kangaroo wants to jump up a tall ledge. The Narrator set this as an challenging task, but Grimsby has a trait that lets him reduce the Difficulty of jumping tasks by 1, pushing it down to to uncommon Difficulty. Grimsbys Might is 2, meaning it is 1 higher than the Difficulty of the task. This grants Grimsby Surge Momentum when attempting to perform this Action.

Example

Grimsby is attempting to jump up a ledge. Before jumping off, he takes a running start. The Narrator rules that this gives Grimsby a Surge Momentum, but because he already has that effect applied through performing an Action of 1 less Difficulty, this second Surge gets discarded. His first roll from 2D10 is a 5, which is not enough to succeed. His second Roll results in a 7. Not much better, but enough to succeed at the task.

Looking up the Result in the table above allows for interpretation of the Outcome. In this step, the Player and Narrator have to cooperate to tell a fitting and narratively satisfying Outcome for this task.

Example

Barbara rolled an 11 when trying to lockpick an old lock, resulting in a Simple Success. Barbara is able to pick the lock easily without any guard nearby being alerted.

The Glossary > Narrator describes how Grimsby takes a running start, jumps off and is able to reach the ledges height, but he jumped too early and now hangs on the edge of the ledge with his paws. Approaches

When a Character is faced with a challenge they are not likely to succeed at, the Player may decide to utilize a fitting Approach. Approaches are a way to boost a Character chances of success against a Glossary > Skill Check that otherwise would result in failure. The Approach does need to be approved by the narrator, as nothing narratively nonsensical should be incorporated into the Story.
When a new Approach is created, it is noted down on the Character Sheet. Upon first use, due to being something the Character is not yet trained in, this Approach can be used only with a Static Modifier of -2. This modifier does not apply on any subsequent uses of this Approach.

Example

A rogue named Syla needs to convince a guard that they were not the one who tried to pickpocket them. The Narrator tells the Player controlling Syla that this is a challenging Charms check. As Syla is terrible in their persuasion, the player asks the Narrator if they could try to distract the guard using their Grace Skill, by throwing a rock against a nearby object unseen as if another Character just ran away. The Narrator allows this creative thinking as an Approach, as this still needs some convincing for the guard to believe there was another person there. So, Sylas Player now may use one Charms die and one Grace die in order to roll this Action. Syla has amazing Grace, granting the Player a D12 instead of the second D4 from a raw Charms check. As this is a new Approach, the Player makes this check but has to roll with a -2 Static Modifier. The two Dice result in a 11 and a 4, which sums up to 15. Subtracting the -2 gives a 13, which is enough for a Simple Success. Syla manages to distract the guard and convince them of there being another person, which gives them a tiny opportunity to flee from the scene.

Helping other characters

It is possible to help out other Characters through the use of a Link Approach. The helping character rolls and Approach with the Link Skill and the Skill Checks required Skill with the Difficulty being 1 less than the original Skill Checks. The Outcome is a Static Modifier on the original Skill Check , whose value is determined through this table:

Outcome Modifier
Critical Fail -4
Simple Fail -2
Complicated Success ±0
Simple Success +2
Critical Success +4

Progress Clocks

When a longer process gets started by one or more Characters, the Narrator may introduce a Progress Clock. These devices are a visual tool for showing long term progress towards some abstract goal.

Creating a Progress Clock

Progress Clocks are usually divided into a number of segments that get filled as Actions related to that Clocks goal succeed or fail. Anytime a segment gets filled with a positive or negative value depending on success or failure, the narrator can have a positive or negative effect occur that will influence the current situation.
Most Progress Clocks follow this format:

  • An overall goal: This is the name of the Progress Clock. It informs about the target being pursued with this Clock.
  • A number of segments: This is the number of actions that have to be performed at most in relation to the goal. At latest when the last segment gets filled in, the overall success on the goal should be evaluated.
  • Time per segment: This number tells how long completing a single segment takes for a single character.
  • A number of successes: This is the threshold of success, at which the goal is generally considered to have a positive turnout. The Characters working on this Progress Clocks must succeed at least this many times to win this Progress Clocks.
  • A number of failures: This is the threshold of failure, at which the goal is generally considered to have a negative turnout. The Characters working on this Progress Clocks must not reach this number of failed actions on the goal to avoid the negative consequences.

    The number of successes and number of failures sum up to the number of segments plus 1.

Creating a Progress Clock

A typical Progress Clock could look like this:
Decypher the encoded letter: 8*1h - 6S/3F
This Progress Clock defines the overall goal to be the decoding of a script. The Clock has 8 segments and the timeframe for each segment is 1 hour, meaning this task will take 8 hours in total to complete. The number of successes is 6 and the number of failures is 3, this means that the characters must achieve at least 6 successes before they reach 3 failures in order to succeed at decyphering this letter.

Advancing the goal

For a Character to advance an existing Progress Clock, the character must perform an Action whose outcome aligns with the Clocks goal. When the Character does that, they roll a normal Skill Check to see whether they advance the goal or not:

  • On a simple success or failure, the result is entered into the clock and the clock advances by one segment.
  • Critical result variants double the number of successes/failures written on the clock and advance the clock by double the amount.

Supporting another character

It is possible for a Character to help out another Character that is currently working on a Progress Clock by rolling a Link Approach with the substituting Skill being the one used by the Character directly working on the Progress Clock. Should the help consist of less direct actions, for example gathering appropriate materials, an appropriate Skill is to be used instead.

Finishing the task

Once the worked on Clock has been filled out completely, the task is completed. How well the Characters did on the task and whether the goal has been accomplished depends on the numbers of successes and failures the Characters have added to the Clock:

  • If the number of required successes has been reached before the number of failures was, the Characters are successful in their task.
  • If the number of maximum failures has been reached before the number of successes was, the Characters fail in their task.
  • If the number of required successes has been surpassed byat least half of the number of failures (rounded down), the Characters succeed critically.
  • If the number of maximum failures has been surpassed by at least half of the number of successes (rounded down), the Characters fail critically.
Succeeding a Progress Clock

The party is tasked with decyphering an encrypted letter. The task can be worked on in 1-hour segments and will take about 8 hours of work. The smartest two Characters share the workload and help each other out to advance the goal faster. After just 4 hours of their combined effort they are able to share their results with the rest of the party. The segments of the Progress Clock are filled in as follows: S-S-S-F-S-S-S-S. This sums up to 1 failure and 7 successes. As they have reached the required number of successes, they do succeed at the task and are able to read the decyphered letter. Even better yet, because they surpassed the number of successes by half of the number of failures rounded down (3/2 = 1.5 -> 1), they succeed critically, thus are able to extract more info out of the letter than expected.

Failing a Progress Clock

The party is tasked with decyphering an encrypted letter. The task can be worked on in 1-hour segments and will take about 8 hours of work. The smartest two Characters share the workload and help each other out to advance the goal faster. After just 4 hours of their combined effort they are able to share their results with the rest of the party. The segments of the Progress Clock are filled in as follows: F-F-S-S-F-F-S-F. This sums up to 3 successes and 5 failures. As they have reached the maximum number of failures, they fail at the task and are not able to read the decyphered letter. Even worse yet, because they surpassed the number of failures by half of the number of successes rounded down (6/2 = 3), they fail critically. The party not only does not get the correct letter content, they manage to somehow misinterpret the whole letter and assume different information based on the wrong results.

Conflicts

Conflicts

Conflicts drive a story. Conflict embodies the core concept of pitting multiple things against each other. In Mortal Engines: XXXXXX, a conflict describes actions taken by multiple characters that have mutually exclusive goals.

Conflict Types

Two types of conflicts arise throughout the game: Oppositions and Encounters.
Oppositions are quickly resolved by all involved characters performing their separate Skill Checks based on the action they want to take. The result dictates the outcome based on the interpretation table:

Encounter Types

Encounters are an umbrella term for multiple types of conflicts that usually are not resolved completely within a few seconds.
Encounters have three types:

  1. Environmental Challenge
  2. Social Parley
  3. Skirmish

Environmental Challenge

Environmental Challenges are most commonly seen on journeys, but they may also be presented within a safe location or indoors. Environmental Challenges usually consist of an obstacle that has to be overcome, but can also be a point of interest that needs to be investigated, or a room with a puzzle that needs to be solved in order to allow the characters to progress.
These challenges are mostly defined through the specific challenge they present the characters, but they all have the following things in common:

  1. Goal: Every challenge comes with a goal that can be reached by performing skill checks and applying clever solutions to problems within the challenge.
  2. Problems: Every challenge also needs obstacles that make it harder for the characters to reach the goal, which the players can solve creatively within the narrators discretion. Failing to overcome these problems may have consequences for the characters.
  3. Complications: Some challenges may apply special complications that make the situation less clear and provide unique threats to circumvent.
Example

The characters have to balance over a fallen tree in oder to cross a river that could harm them severely. They are followed by a group of hungry wolfes, who are not yet in line of sight but have followed their scents.
The goal in this case is to reach the other side, in order to flee from the wolfes. The problem is the river, with an obvious possible solution being presented. The consequences of failing to balance the tree can range from simply getting wet to even be sucked under the surface from the water stream and be injured severely. The wolfes act as a complication in the form of a timer, where too much stalling would result in a combat encounter rather than a successful escape.

Example

A character discoveres a corpse hidden under a pile of books inside a library, with an ornate wooden crate next to the scene.
The goal is defined as finding out what happened. The problem is not having enough information to deduce anything useful, with the consequence of failure being deducting the wrong results. Although not presented in this case, a potential obstacle could be a police force already investigating the scene and hiding away valuable evidence.

Social Parley

When characters meet another, a social parley can begin. Here, similar to environmental challenges, characters are tasked to reach a goal that lies behind a problem, with potential complications in the mix. These elements are however more of metaphysical nature, and so most parleys are held off using mental skills.

Example

One character arrives at a market to buy a certain rare item.
The goal is to obtain the item and return to the party, with the problem being a haggle that is about to ensue.
A complication may arise in the form of another customer, who also shows interest in the same rare item.

Skirmish

Skirmishes are defined by two or more factions trying to defeat each other through violent means. A faction is defined as one or more characters with the same goal within this encounter. Should the goal of a character shift within the encounter, that character remains within the faction for the rest of the encounter.
At the start of Skirmishes, the narrator defines the factions based on their common goals. Skirmishes change the game into a turn-based setup, where all factions take turns performing actions to achieve their own goals.

Tip

To quicken this process, in most circumstances preplanned encounters can have their factions predefined, and most parties can be summed as one faction.

In general, the faction that opens the skirmish by performing the first attack begins the battle. Then, each faction rolls a D20 to determine the order of turns. All factions are now performing their actions and reactions in that incremental order, with the first turn done by the faction that opened the battle. Should a character gain a surprise round on one or more other characters, those surprised can not react to the surprising characters actions.

Example

The party of 4 players is sent to obtain a high ranking artifact from a nearby small military convoy. One of the members notices the convoy being ambushed by a group of bandits. They manage to sneak up, just as the bandits exit their hiding spots to surround the convoy.
Then, the party launches a surprise attack against the bandits. A skirmish starts, and the factions are declared: The bandits all have the common goal to rob the convoy of all their valuables, putting them all into one faction. The players are all trying to somehow obtain the artifact, placing them in another faction. The convoy, who tries to fend off both factions, is the third faction.
All factions now roll a D20 to determine the turn order. The player faction, who started the conflict, acts first. Their roll is a 7. The convoy rolls a 15, and the bandits roll a 1. Going in incremental looping order, after the players are done the convoy acts, and then the bandits get their turn.

Skirmish Combat

Faction turns

All members of a faction share a simultaneously happening turn, during which each character gets 2 actions and 2 reactions. Each action and each reaction takes about 2 to 3 seconds to perform.
Actions are spent during a characters factions own turn, while reactions are spent outside of that turn.

Within a Skirmish, characters may choose to perform different actions based on their position, their equipment, or their skills.
While equipment does offer more equipment specific actions, every character has access to a list of core actions. These core actions include:

  • Move (RFX): This action lets the character move a moderate distance, adding to or subtracting from this distance based on the characters RFX skill.
  • Attack with weapon: This action uses a currently held weapon to injure to a target within weapon reach. The action skill depends on the weapon the character uses.
  • Attack without weapon (STR): This action uses a characters unarmed body to strike a target within melee reach.
  • Interact: This action is a catch-all action for any activity not included in the standard list that can be performed without any special equipment. The action skill depends on the specific action the character performs.
  • Support: This action lets the character notice another character attempting a difficult task, and offering their help.
  • Dodge (RFX) (Reaction): This reaction lets the character dodge an incoming attack to take no damage. Worn equipment may reduce effectivity of this action.
  • Block (STR) (Reaction): This reaction lets the character block an incoming attack to take less damage. Worn equipment may increase effectivity of this action.
  • Interact (Reaction): This reaction is a catch-all reaction for any activity not included in the standard list that can be performed without any special equipment. The reaction skill depends on the specific reaction the character performs.
  • Support (Reaction): This reaction lets the character notice another character attempting a difficult task, and offering their help.
    Held items can also be used to take combat actions, although these actions are not listed here but explained in the items section.

Action "Move (RFX)":

The Move action is the most basic action a character can take. They can use this to reposition themself in a battle, hide behind cover, sneak up on someone, or simply evade an area. When a character takes the move action, they are able to relocate themselves up to a certain distance for this one action. This distance is known as their movement speed, which depends on the characters abilities, equipment and lineage.
By default, characters are able to move in 4 cardinal directions, they can jump down ledges, and climb climbable surfaces or objects to move up or down in the environment. The characters feats, talents and lineage may influence these basic movement options.
Typically, movement is calculated in metric units using meters as the base line.

Action "Attack":

For the purpose of simplicity, this paragraph explains the rules for attacking both with and without a weapon.

When a character makes an attack against a target within (weapon) reach, they choose whether to attack cautiously or recklessly. A cautious attack reduces the resulting injury severity while granting a surge, and a reckless attack increases the injury severity while granting a stall.
The attacker also chooses the hit zone they want to attack on the target. The target may perform a reaction in response to the attack, if they are able and allowed to. If the target does not react, the narrator sets the difficulty for the action. If the target does react, the resulting opposition gets resolved like any other opposition, with each character rolling their respective skill dice and adding static modifiers, then comparing the result using the defender as the difficulty level:

  • If the attacker fails the attack, the attack misses, or the target dodges or blocks successfully, depending on the targets reaction.
  • If the attacker rolls the targets number, the attacker deals one strain.
  • If the attacker rolls between 1 and 4 higher than the target, the attacker deals one wound.
  • If the attacker rolls more than 4 higher than the target, the attacker deals one trauma.

Hit Zones
Every character has 3 defined hit zones: The bulk, the brim, and the ace.
When a character tries to physically attack another character, the attacker declares the hit zone they are going for. Depending on the targets body, each hit zone can have one or more areas.

  • The bulk is the center of mass on a target. This zone is the easiest to hit, but it does not offer any further benefits.
  • The ace is the targets weak point. With humans, this usually is the head. Attempting to hit this zone increases increases the hit threshold by 2. Hitting this zone increases the damage severity by one tier.
  • The brim consists of any other limbs that are attached to the bulk or any other limbs. Limbs can be targeted to disable them until they are treated. Attempting to hit this zone increases the hit threshold by 1. Hitting a limb disables that limb and any other limbs that are attached to the bulk through that limb.

Action "Interact":

This action defines any action that can not be placed under any other directly defined action. The boundaries and limitations for the characters actions underly the narrators discretion. As guidelines, here are helpful tips to make this process easier for both parties:

  • One turn takes about 5 seconds in total based on the number of default actions and reactions each character is able to perform. Therefore, if a character wishes to do something that takes longer than that, it would likely take more than one round to complete.
  • Depending on the characters equipment, positioning, and surroundings, the player may have unique ideas for how to interact with their environment.
  • A character may use an interact action to generate progress on a currently active progress clock, which segments do not take longer than 2 actions each.
  • The difficulty of this action lies at the narrators discretion, like a common skill check.

Reaction "Support":

This action represents the character recognizing another character trying to accomplish a difficult goal and offering their help. The character may perform a temporary Approach Skill Check at the same time as the character they try to help at the same difficulty using the Synergy skill and the most appropriate skill for this check. When doing this, their roll acts as a Surge momentum for the character they are supporting.

Reaction "Dodge":

This reaction represents a character evading a direct incoming attack to prevent damage from being taken. As a reaction, the character may only perform this outside their own turn. Dodging uses the RFX skill. This reaction is resolved like a normal Skill Check with no additional modifier:

  • On a critical success, the character manages to evade the incoming attack so much that it decreases the resulting injury by 2 tiers.
  • On a simple success, the character manages to evade the incoming attack so much that it decreases the resulting injury by 1 tier.
  • On a complicated success, the player may choose to reduce the incoming injury by 1 tier at the cost of gaining Stall momentum on their next action or any similar negative draw.
  • On a simple fail, the character does not manage to evade the incoming attack, and gains Stall momentum on their next action or any similar negative draw.
  • On a critical fail, the character evades so badly that they directly either increase this incoming injury by 1 tier or they gain a second Strain tier injury.

Constitution and Resilience

Vitality

Severity tiers
Incoming harm is measured in severity tiers, both for injuries and stress.
For injuries, these severities are:

  • Strain
  • Wound
  • Trauma
    For Stress, these severities are:
  • Agitation
  • Panic
  • Burnout

An injury on a leg could, depending on the severity tier, be anything from a scratch, to a broken bone, to a dismemberment.

Vitality refers to a characters ability to withstand some amount of harm, both physically and mentally. Vitality is measured in constitution for physical harm and resilience for mental harm.

Measuring Vitality

When a character receives harm, be it stress or an injury, this has to be noted on the character sheet to track it over multiple scenes, chapters and in extreme cases even arcs and whole stories. This harm affects how much more a character can take. As long as these harms are not treated and recovered from, the character will be at higher risk of succumbing to a falling unconscious, mental collapseor dying outright.
Vitality is tracked in two ways that play into each other: Through noting the different harms in a harm list, and through tracking active harm tokens.
The number of Vitality tokens a character has is mostly determined through a characters lineage. This lineage lists 6 different token numbers, each depicting the number of tokens of one specific severity tier of either harm type. At the start of a new story, players each place the correct numbers of tokens according to their characters lineage on the character sheets Vitality area.

Example

An average human (lineage "Human") has the following Vitality values:

  • 3 Strain
  • 1 Wound
  • 0 Trauma
  • 3 Agitation
  • 1 Panic
  • 0 Burnout

This makes any player with a human character gain 3 tier 1 injury tokens, 3 tier 1 stress tokens, 1 tier 2 token for both injuries and stress each, but no tier 3 tokens.

Using Vitality tokens

Vitality tokens are used to mark fresh injuries and uncalmed stress on the character sheet. The details of this process of adding the harm to the character sheet and assigning a Vitality token is different between injuries and stress.

​Injuries

Injuries can be obtained in two ways: By being hurt by other characters or by failing physical skill checks. When a character gets hurt by another character, the result of the attack is considered for deciding the initial severity, see Attack Actions for detail. On environmental damage, the severity depends on how hard the character fails their skill check.

Outcome Severity
Critical Fail Wound
Simple Fail Strain
Complicated Success -
Simple Success -
Critical Success -
Tip

Success with complications can give Strain injuries, but this should be used only if the narrator can not think of better complications. It is generally more fun to come up with narratively impactful consequences to a character barely making it.

Receiving injuries

When a character receives an injury of a given severity, they go through the following process:

1. Checking Resistances and Vulnerabilities

Some characters withstand certain types of physical damage more easily or suffer types of physical damage more intensely. These interactions are called Resistances and Vulnerabilities respectively.
When a character gets injured with a certain severity, the player checks to see if they have resistances or vulnerabilities against the incoming damage types.
They sum up each of the interactions and subtract the number of resistances from the number of vulnerabilities, and then modify the severity of the incoming injury according to that number:

  • If the number is negative, they reduce the severity by this amount of tiers.
    Should an injury by this process be reduced to less than a strain, it gets dropped.
  • If the number is positive, they increase the severity by the number of tiers.
    Should an injury by this process be escalated to more than a Trauma, the character immediately enters the Dying state.
Tip

Incoming attacks should not have too many unique damage types to not bloat the process of checking resistances and vulnerabilities. Likewise, custom lineages should not have too many resistances and vulnerabilities as well for the same reason.

Reducing severity

Barry the athlete is currently competing in a race. He stumbled and fell, earning him an injury. Usually, this injury is a strain. The applied severity of this incoming injury depends on the damage type interactions. As Barry stumbles and falls to the ground, this is a scratch wound. Barry is not a human, however, but a character with a fictional lineage, which grant him resistance on bludgeoning damage due to their tough skin. The narrator allows the player to apply this resistance to the incoming damage. The injury gets reduced by one severity tier for each applicable resistance, which reduces the injury below a strain - letting the player ignore this injury outright.

Increasing severity

Barry the athlete takes part in a sword fight. He gets caught at the shoulder by his enemies blade. The severity of this injury is usually a Strain, but as Barry is vulnerable to cutting damage, the severity automatically increases to a Wound.

After applying resistances and vulnerabilities to an injury, and the injury severity exists somewhere from Strain to Trauma, the player writes down that injury with the given severity and optionally the injury state ("A" for active) in the harm list on the character sheet. If the player can not add any more harm to the list due to it being full, the player may choose to have their character fall unconscious or enter the dying state.

2. Placing a Vitality token

Next, the player removes one of their Constitution tokens of at least that injury severity to place on the newly added injury. If the player does not have a token of that severity, they look for a free token of the next higher severity and so on, until they either run out of tokens or can place one on the new injury. The player can at any time they need a Constitution token sacrifice a D8 on a physical attribute to substitute a Strain token, a D10 on a physical attribute to substitute a Wound token, or a D12 on a physical attribute to substitute a Trauma token, as long as the player has 2 dice of that magnitude left in that attribute.

3. Fallout

Should the player have sacrificed one of their D8s, D10s or D12s from a physical attribute on an injury, they are unable to perform any skill checks with only that attribute. Instead, they have to use an approach with another attribute. An approach can utilize multiple impaired attributes.

Healing injuries

While injuries do not immediately affect the direct gameplay from moment to moment, they tend to affect the games upcoming scenes in a cascading way. It is thus very important to remove injuries as soon as possible so a character stays alive as long as possible.

Treating an injury

After an injury is added to the character sheet, it can be treated haphazardly. This is unique for every injury type, but usually involves some quickly improvised equipment, like a ripped apart shirt to function as makeshift bandages, or a pocket knife being repurposed into a terrible and unsanitary scalpel when no better cutting tools are available.
When a player manages to have their character get one of their injuries treated, the player may move the Constitution token back to the Vitality graphic or reclaim the sacrificed dice into their dice pool, and change the injury state from "A" to "T" for "treated". This will not remove the injury outright, but enables a slow natural healing process for that injury. It also allows the player to roll raw skill checks with the attribute they needed that dice for again.

Curing an injury

Injuries can be removed from the character sheet in two ways: Natural healing and medical treatment.
Natural healing happens over time, while medical treatment can be done up to once per scene per character as long as the characters can afford it with adequate medical equipment and access to attribute and knowledge in that area.

Natural healing

Natural healing takes place during downtime:

  • At the end of a Rest, any resting player may remove 1 Strain injury from their harm list, giving back any not recollected Constitution tokens.
  • At the end of a Respite, they may remove all Strain and 1 Wound injury from their harm list, giving back any not recollected Constitution tokens.
  • Trauma does not get removed through natural healing and always needs medical treatment to be cured.
Medical treatment

When a character seeks medical treatment, they need appropriate resources in the form of medical equipment, drugs and characters that are skilled in medical treatment of that kind.
Once a character receives medical treatment for a specific injury, the player may take back any Constitution tokens and sacrificed dice from that injury immediately and change the state of that injury from "A" or "T" to "C" to remove it at the end of the next reclusion.

Stress

Stress can be obtained in two ways: By being mentally hurt by other characters or by failing mental skill checks. When a character gets mentally hurt by another character, the result of the attack is considered for deciding the initial severity. On social damage, the severity depends on how hard the character fails their skill check.

Outcome Severity
Critical Fail Panic
Simple Fail Agitation
Complicated Success -
Simple Success -
Critical Success -
Tip

Success with complications can give Agitation stress, but this should be used only if the narrator can not think of better complications. It is generally more fun to come up with narratively impactful consequences to a character barely making it.

Receiving stress

When a character receives stress of a given severity, they go through the following process:

1. Adding stress to the list of injuries

First, the player writes down the type of stress their character received with the severity tier. Next, the player removes one of their Resilience tokens of at least that stress severity to place on the newly added stress. If the player does not have a token of that severity, they look for a free token of the next higher severity and so on, until they either run out of tokens or can place one on the new stress. Should the player not have any free Resilience tokens of at least that severity left, they may sacrifice a D8 on a mental attribute as an Agitation token, a D10 on a mental attribute as a Panic token, or a D12 on a mental attribute as a Burnout token. If that is also not possible anymore, the character enters the dying state.

3. Fallout

Should the player have placed one of their D8s, D10s or D12s from a mental attribute on any stress, they are unable to complete any raw skill checks with only that attribute. Instead, they have to use an approach with another attribute. Normal approach rules apply here.

Recovering from stress

Unlike injuries, stress can not be treated through medical care. Instead, it takes time spent in safety and among good company to heal stress.
Outside of skirmishes, other characters may approach a stressed out character and provide some peptalk. This action results in a raw Synergy skill check of the approaching character. The outcome of that attribute check depends on the result of that skill check:

Result Outcome
Critical Fail Add another Panic
Simple Fail Add another Agitation
Complicated Success Nothing happens
Simple Success Remove 1 Agitation
Critical Success Remove 1 Panic or 2 Agitations

Other than having people to talk to, holding downtime in a safe area also decreases stress over time:

  • At the end of a rest the player may remove 1 Agitation.
  • At the end of a respite, the player may remove all Agitations and 1 Panic.
  • At the end of a reclusion, the player may remove all stress from the characters resilience.

Losing a character

Should a character suffer too much harm at once, it leads to breaching limits. Characters have three limits that can be breached:

  1. running out of injury tokens breaches the constitution
  2. running out of stress tokens breaches the resilience
  3. running out of space on the harm list breaches the endurance limit.

Each of these limit breaches lead to a default outcome for the player, but if narratively justifiable, the player may take a different option.
The default options for each of these cases are:

  1. Breaching consitution
    The character gains a final surge, a last stand during which nothing will stand in their way. For 5 seconds or 1 faction turn, any skill checks the character would have performed are considered a critical success. After this timespan the character dies irrevocably and is removed from the story permanently.
  2. Breaching resilience
    The characters grip on reality slips. From now on, the character gets 6 final actions. Each time the character performs an action, the player rolls a D6. If that die roll is higher than the number of remaining actions, the character fully lost their mind and becomes unresponsive to outside influences.
  3. Breaching endurance
    The character has endured too much over a long period of time, they are unable to continue on like this, the body automatically takes emergency measures. The player picks one of their character's most severe harms in the endurance list. This harm now becomes unhealable and the state gets changed to "P" for permanent. If a token is placed on the harm, it gets voided. The character stays unresponsive for the remainder of the scene, but does not get removed from the story. After the scene ends, the player regains control over the character.

Weapons, Armor and Equipment

Equipment

During skirmishes, carrying weapons and wearing armor has oftentimes proven effective to amplify ones chances of success for reaching their goal, be it defeating other characters, invading a base, or simply surviving a battle. In 5D, weapons and armor are pieces of equipment that a character has in their inventory.

Weapons

Weapons are the main way of increasing both the reach of an attack damage output of said attack. To track weapons effectively and efficiently, they all fall into this scheme:

  • Name: every weapon has an identifier. By default it is a descriptor of the weapon, eg "Boxing Gloxes", but players are free to rename weapons however they like, as long as it is clear what weapon they are talking about.
  • Type (Skill): every weapon that is used to attack relies on some form of wielder skill. For melee weapons, this is usually Might, ranged weapons usually use Agility. Advanced weapons that don't require manual input typically require Wits, and magical weapons might rely on Charms.
  • Reach: the effective range the weapon is meant to be used in. If the target is too close, the attack automatically stalls. If the target is too far and the weapon is a melee weapon, the attack can not hit. If the target is too far and the weapon is a ranged weapon, the attack automatically stalls. By default, there are 5 different ranges:
    Grapple, Melee, Long, Far, and Artillery.

    Miniature Fights only:
    Which range the distance between a character and the target falls under is determined by these measurement methods:
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the narrators pinky fingers width, the range is Grapple.
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the narrators index fingers and pinky fingers combined, the range is Melee.
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the distance from the narrators index pinky finger to the index finger, the range is Long.
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the narrators maximum span of a single hand, the range is Far.
    Anything outside that distance falls under Artillery.

  • Grip: most weapons need to be held in some form. Every weapon lists what kind of grip is necessary to use it properly. Improper grip can be used, if narratively justifiable, but results in a stall. By default, there are 3 different grips:
    One Handed, Two Handed and Twinned.
  • Damage Types: every attack uses some sort of damage type. Resistances and Vulnerabilities against these damage types change the injury severity on the target. By default, there are 3 different damage types:
    Blunt, Cut, Pierce
  • Extra Traits: any important additional info about the weapon gets noted down here. Example on a Twinned Grip Sable: "Twin Blades: when used with a second Theft Blade and the character has a vocation for using weapons, attacks automatically surge".

Armor

While weapons can prove useful to afflict stronger injuries on an opponent, armor helps protecting against too severe injuries.
Armor comes in three coverage tiers, each protecting more than the previous one:

  • half armor, when worn, will cover the bulk of the wearer.
  • full armor, when worn, will cover the bulk and appendages of the wearer but decrease dodging by -2
  • closed armor is made up of full armor and an ace piece, which covers the wearers weak point but decreases dodging by -4.
    Along with these three cover tiers, armor comes in different strength levels:
  • basic armor adds a static modifier of +2 for blocking.
  • refined armor adds a static modifier of +4 for blocking.
  • fortified armor adds a static modifier of +6 for blocking.

The Character Sheet

Character Sheet

Characters in 5D consist of a multitude of facettes. All of them fall into either of two categories, which are the gameplay properties and the narrative properties.

Narrative

On a narrative level, each character has an identity and a characterization of this person. The identity and characterization form the backbone of how a character acts and views the world around them in any given situation.

Identity

Property Explanation
Name How the character gets referred to by others
Age How long this character has been roaming earth
Origin Where that character has been born
Characteristics Mechanically not significant details to the character, ie changes in looks, behavioral ticks, etc

The identity of a character consists of statistical data to describe measurable information about this character.
This consists of the name and age of the character, as well as their origin and characteristics.
The origin is a characters home place. It tells where the character was born, what kind of life they likely have lived, and pushes a probable belief system onto the character, which is based on the society they are a part of.
The characteristics are a list of detail facts about a character. They can range from noticeable appearance changes, to behavioral ticks. These characteristics do not have any mechanical impact on the gameplay, they serve as roleplay hooks to make the character more interesting.

Characterization

Facette Related question
Backstory How did the character come to be who and where they are now?
Goals What is the characters current dream they wish to achieve?
Beliefs What does the character believe in? Do they follow a strict moral code of some higher authority or rather make it up as they go along?

The characterization section consists of a characters background story, their goals and aspirations, and their beliefs and morals.
The background story aims to explain briefly why and how that character got to the point they are at at the beginning of the story.
The goals and aspirations inform what a character wants to accomplish in their life. This can be a long term goal like "seeing the entire world", or a number of smaller milestones like "trade with different nationalities salesmen" and "buy my own airship".
The beliefs and morals of a character give guidelines for how a character views the world and how they interact with it. This is influenced a lot by the origin society, as they typically lay a ground work for what a character gets served as facts, and what they are allowed to accept as truth.

Tip

This section is deliberately open-ended. When answering each of these questions, try to be creative, but do not be afraid of writing down simpler and easier to understand answers.

Gameplay

On a gameplay level a character has differently scored abilities. Additionally, every character has one to two vocations they are trained at, which when applicable provide a bonus to a required skill.
Likewise, a character holds an amount of some currency in their purse, holds, wears and carries unique equipment in their hands, holsters and pockets, and has suffered different injuries, each with distinct effects and (dis-)abilities. This section is referred to as the characters Status.

Abilities

Abilities lie at the core of everything the character can do to a certain extend. They inform about the strengths and weaknesses of the character, and what areas of expertise they can excel at.

Skills

Every character has access to five skills. These are:

  • Might (M):
    Might is useful for being able to sustain more bodily harm, displaying athletic talent, or hitting things harder.
  • Agility (A):
    Agility gets used for dodging attacks, piloting a machine, or performing acrobatic feats.
  • Wits (W)):
    Wits represents the characters intelligence. This includes their ability to deduce, being more able to craft objects together, or holding a lot of knowledge in their memory.
  • Charms (C):
    Charms displays the ability to trick and persuade other characters, and to read another characters true intentions.
  • Synergy (S):
    Synergy means the characters ability to be supportive towards others, to provide comfort and be a good person.

Character Creation

Character Creation

A character within 5Dice can be created within minutes of having a good idea. Using the following process you can manifest your dream easily.

1. Inspiration

It all starts with a simple idea, known as the pitch. A pitch is a narrative hook that draws the attention of the audience into the pitched idea by providing an interesting twist to an otherwise potentially mundane thought. The pitch should be no longer than two sentences.
From this pitch a concept can be formed. The concept is more concrete than the pitch in terms of how that character fits into the world, but it is not as constricting as choosing a city to be born in.
This concept is the backbone of your character design, and on a narrative level as many elements of the character creation process as possible should conform to support this core idea.

2. Becoming

After finding a concept, it is time to fill in the backstory of the character. The backstory tells about a characters past, and the reasons why they are in their current position. It is recommended to keep the backstory simple and written in bullet points for easier reference.

3. Identity

With the backstory you also know where the character stands in their own life. Now you can fill in the characters identity, which consists of their name, age, origin, and characteristics.
The character origin refers to the place they were born.
Characteristics mean special features of the character that are minor tweaks to the appearance or behavior.

4. Goals

After the backstory it is time for the goals and aspirations. You know where the character came from, now you need to decide on where they want to go. A character can have a single goal or multiple goals. It is recommended to keep the list of goals as short as possible. Goals represent achievements that are to the character worth pursuing for their intrinsic values rather for external motivation.

5. Morals

A characters beliefs guide the character throughout the world with a filter of morale through which the character forms a certain world view. These morals are most easily influenced through the characters upbringing, i.e. their Becoming. From a characters origin their beliefs can be inferred, and the initial moral is copied from the kind of society they are a part of.

6. Vocations

7. Attributes

After the narrative part of the character is created, the gameplay section can be filled in. Using the notes from the previously filled areas, decide on how to spend the characters skill points. On tier 1, a character has 2 skill points, and on tier 2 a character has 4 skill points.
All core skills of any character start at 0 skill points, which translates into a D6.
Skill points are used on skills to increase the dice value by 2 (to a maximum of 3 with a resulting D12), or a skill point may be gained by reducing one D6 to a D4.

The second part of the abilities are the professions. Every character must additionally have at least a major profession and may have a minor profession at the cost of 1 skill point. The major profession is their way of earning their daily bread, be it by playing music for others, accounting finances, robbing people on the streets or hunting animals in the wild.
The minor profession can be either a hobby, a side project that has nothing to do with the major profession, or it could even be a former job that still left some knowledge.
Professions are used during skill checks where applicable major professions grant a highroll to the result and applicable minor professions add +2 to the result.

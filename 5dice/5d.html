5D

Introduction and game preparation

This roleplaying game is a Setting-agnostic cooperative storytelling System that is played by at least 2 people. The game can be adapted to fit a multitude of different Settings. In a game of 5D, Players come together to collectively tell a Story within an agreed upon Setting using Roleplaying as the main driver behind Character choices, while Outcomes of Actions with uncertain Results are determined mostly through Dice Rolls. One Player known as the Narrator describes the World of the Story and important elements in the surroundings of the Player Characters, which are controlled by the other Players. Each Player may take control over one or more Characters, with any left over Characters being taken over by the Narrator.

Required Materials

Every Player needs to have access to two full sets of standard polymetric Dice, including the D4, D6, D8, D10, D12, D20 and D%). Furthermore, all Players should have read and understood the rules that are relevant to their Role, including any Setting specific rules which can alter the game.

Preparation

Setting specific preparation

Every Setting follows the core rules laid out in this document, but may add their specific Mechanics and Subsystems to enhance or alternate the experience. In case a Setting requires more preparation than is listed here, the Setting is listing the preparation steps needed separately in its own section.

After all Players can agree on a specific type of Story, they collectively decide who should be the Narrator, the rest will continue asCharacters. Every Player may take control over one or more Characters within the story to dictate those Characters Actions. These picked Characters are called Player Characters. The Narrator is a special Role among the Players. They are responsible for describing the World the Story takes place in and the Actions performed by the Player Characters and how they affect the environment.
In principal, every Player should begin the game with a certain goal in mind, something they wish to achieve during this game. This goal helps inform the narrator on where to take the Story.

Once the bearer of the Narrator Role has been decided, the Narrator can begin the game preparation.
Preparing a game of 5D is much like planning out certain events of a Story. A Story is typically structured into one or more Arcs, each divided into Chapters, which in turn can be divided into one or more Scenes.

Picking a Chapter goal

When starting preparation of a new game of 5D, the narrator need only to pick a Chapter goal, as this is usually the amount of Story that a game can provide within a single session. This Chapter goal is informed by the overarching currently active Narrative and the major plot points who are driving the Story forward. The Narrator is also advised to take the Players goals into consideration at this point, to increase Player engagement. The goal can be newly created or a preexisting one from a previous adventure.

Assessing the situation

After the goal is defined, next up the Narrator should assess the current situation of the main party, where they are located and what they are planning to do. This is the point where game preparation can differ wildly depending on whether the Players are starting a new Story or continuing a previous adventure.
If the Players continue a previous adventure, the narrator is advised to revisit the scenes of the last game, to inform themselves of where the Players left off. This takes off a lot of the prep work on this step required, as it already establishes who the Characters are, how they met and what they each want to accomplish in the story at this point. Should the Players start a new Story, they have to collectively come up with all the answers to this from scratch. It is helpful to answer these questions in order to begin a new Story:

  • Who is each character?
  • What do they have in common?
  • What differentiates them?
  • How did they first meet?

Providing options

The Narrator may want to add a few different options in order to achieve the goal the Players have set out to accomplish, which ideally cover different play styles to ensure Players have different viable choices to pick their favorite path from. Finally, to map out the beats of the Story, the Narrator should prepare a few core scenes that will be key in achieving the goal through one or more options.

Mapping out the beginning

Finally, when the goal is known and the path there roughly mapped out, the Narrator can connect back with the Players and figure out an opening Scene.

Playing the game

Structure

By the nature of the game, 5D is best used for telling a Story over one or more sessions.
Like any Story, its structure can be broken down into Arcs, each being made up of Chapters, which in turn can be broken down even further into Scenes.
All of these Story chunks can be as large or small as the Narrator deems fit. They can be a tiny throw-away line skipping a whole Chapter, or they could use a few hours on a single Scene.

Skill Checks

Skill Checks

Skill Checks are a tool to determine the Outcome of any interaction with not predetermined Results. They can be used whenever a Character attempts something and the Outcome is not fully known.
A Skill Check always follows a simple structure: First, the Narrator determines the Skill to be used and the Difficulty of the task. Then, the Player rolls the required Dice and sums up the Result, using any applicable Modifiers. Finally, the Narrator and the Player work together to interpret the Result into an Outcome that fits within the Narrative of the game.

Determining the Difficulty

Value Name
0 Trivial
1 Uncommon
2 Challenging
3 Extreme
4 Impossible
5 Inconceivable

In order for a Character to see how well they do on a given Skill Check, the Narrator has to set the Difficulty. This Difficulty is a numeric value ranging from 0 to 5 that increases linearly with rising difficulty of the task. The name of that numerical value indicates the intended Difficulty of that Skill Check for an average Character of that Lineage within the Setting.

Example

Barbara wants to pick a lock. Because the lock is quite old and posesses a rather simple mechanism, the Narrator sets this Skill Check as an Uncommon Grace task.

Example

Grimsby is trying to jump up a tall ledge. The narrator sets the Skill Check as a Challenging Might task. Since Grimsby is a kangaroo and not a human, he gets to reduce the Difficulty by 1 tier for jumping related actions as part of his Lineage. Thus, Grimsby has to succeed at an Uncommon task rather than a Challenging one to succeed in this Action.

Assembling the DicePool

Once the Narrator announces the Skill used for the task, the Player can pick up the required dice to roll in the check depending on the Characters training level in that Skill:

Skill Points Dice
-1 (Inept) 2D4
0 (Novice) 2D6
1 (Journeyman) 2D8
2 (Expert) 2D10
3 (Master) 2D12
Example

Barbara tries to pick the lock. Her Grace is at +1, giving her access to a 2D8 Dice Pool.

Example

Grimsby has 2 Skill Points in Might, allowing him to roll 2D10 on his attempt to jump up the ledge.

Difficulty and Skills

Depending on the task at hand, the Difficulty can vary. Oftentimes, the task does not exactly match the Characters Skill level perfectly, which offsets the two values.
Depending on the constellation of these two variable tiers, different Skill Check Modifiers apply automatically:

Character Skill - Task Difficulty Modification
-2 or less Static -5
-1 Stall momentum
0 Normal roll
1 Surge momentum
2 or more Static +2 or automatic simple success

Apart from outright failing or succeeding a task, two more types of Modifiers exist that can change the Outcome of a Skill Check: Momentum and Static Modifiers.

Momentum
Rolling with Momentum means rolling the same Skill Check twice, and taking the higher or lower Result, depending on whether the Character is Surging (higher result) or Stalling (lower result).
A Character is Surging once at least one active effect grants them Surge, but no Stalling effect is applied.
Likewise, a Character is Stalling once at least one active effect applies Stall to them, but no Surging effect is granted.
Should a Character have any combination of at least one Surging and at least one Stalling effect applied to them, they cancel each other out and the Character does not gain any of these effects.

Static Modifiers
Static Modifiers are a way to adjust the Result of a roll and adding a small number to it, be it positive or negative.
After the Dice Roll (including a potential Momentum roll) is made, the player adds all applicable Static Modifiers to the rolled number, forming a final Result.

Example

Barbara wishes to pick a lock with a simple mechanism. The Narrator decided that this is an uncommon task. Barbaras Skill Level is 1, which matches the task Difficulty. She thus has to roll the check once.

Example

Barbara tries to pick an old lock using her lockpicking equipment. This picklock grants her a simple +1 on lockpicking Actions. Barbara uses the 2D8 from her Skill and rolls a 10. The picklocks Static Modifier boosts her result to 11.

Example

Grimsby the kangaroo wants to jump up a tall ledge. The Narrator set this as an challenging task, but Grimsby has a trait that lets him reduce the Difficulty of jumping tasks by 1, pushing it down to to uncommon Difficulty. Grimsbys Might is 2, meaning it is 1 higher than the Difficulty of the task. This grants Grimsby Surge Momentum when attempting to perform this Action.

Example

Grimsby is attempting to jump up a ledge. Before jumping off, he takes a running start. The Narrator rules that this gives Grimsby a Surge Momentum, but because he already has that effect applied through performing an Action of 1 less Difficulty, this second Surge gets discarded. His first roll from 2D10 is a 5, which is not enough to succeed. His second Roll results in a 7. Not much better, but enough to succeed at the task.

Interpreting the result

Result Outcome
3 or less Critical Fail
4 to 6 Simple Fail
7 to 9 Complicated Success
10 to 14 Simple Success
15+ Critical Success

Looking up the Result in the table above allows for interpretation of the Outcome. In this step, the Player and Narrator have to cooperate to tell a fitting and narratively satisfying Outcome for this task.

Example

Barbara rolled an 11 when trying to lockpick an old lock, resulting in a Simple Success. Barbara is able to pick the lock easily without any guard nearby being alerted.

Example

Grimsby achieved a 7 when trying to jump up the ledge. This barely reaches the a Complicated Success. The Player offers to take an Injury as Complication from misjudging the jump. The Glossary > Narrator describes how Grimsby takes a running start, jumps off and is able to reach the ledges height, but he jumped too early and now hangs on the edge of the ledge with his paws. The hard jolt on his arms strains the muscles and bones, and Grimsby has received a Strain through this Glossary > Action.

Approaches

When a Character is faced with a challenge they are not likely to succeed at, the Player may decide to utilize a fitting Approach. Approaches are a way to boost a Character chances of success against a Glossary > Skill Check that otherwise would result in failure. The Approach does need to be approved by the narrator, as nothing narratively nonsensical should be incorporated into the Story.
When a new Approach is created, it is noted down on the Character Sheet. Upon first use, due to being something the Character is not yet trained in, this Approach can be used only with a Static Modifier of -2. This modifier does not apply on any subsequent uses of this Approach.

Example

A rogue named Syla needs to convince a guard that they were not the one who tried to pickpocket them. The Narrator tells the Player controlling Syla that this is a challenging Charms check. As Syla is terrible in their persuasion, the player asks the Narrator if they could try to distract the guard using their Grace Skill, by throwing a rock against a nearby object unseen as if another Character just ran away. The Narrator allows this creative thinking as an Approach, as this still needs some convincing for the guard to believe there was another person there. So, Sylas Player now may use one Charms die and one Grace die in order to roll this Action. Syla has amazing Grace, granting the Player a D12 instead of the second D4 from a raw Charms check. As this is a new Approach, the Player makes this check but has to roll with a -2 Static Modifier. The two Dice result in a 11 and a 4, which sums up to 15. Subtracting the -2 gives a 13, which is enough for a Simple Success. Syla manages to distract the guard and convince them of there being another person, which gives them a tiny opportunity to flee from the scene.

Helping other characters

It is possible to help out other Characters through the use of a Link Approach. The helping character rolls and Approach with the Link Skill and the Skill Checks required Skill with the Difficulty being 1 less than the original Skill Checks. The Outcome is a Static Modifier on the original Skill Check , whose value is determined through this table:

Outcome Modifier
Critical Fail -4
Simple Fail -2
Complicated Success ±0
Simple Success +2
Critical Success +4

Progress Clocks

When a longer process gets started by one or more Characters, the Narrator may introduce a Progress Clock. These devices are a visual tool for showing long term progress towards some abstract goal.

Creating a Progress Clock

Progress Clocks are usually divided into a number of segments that get filled as Actions related to that Clocks goal succeed or fail. Anytime a segment gets filled with a positive or negative value depending on success or failure, the narrator can have a positive or negative effect occur that will influence the current situation.
Most Progress Clocks follow this format:

  • An overall goal: This is the name of the Progress Clock. It informs about the target being pursued with this Clock.
  • A number of segments: This is the number of actions that have to be performed at most in relation to the goal. At latest when the last segment gets filled in, the overall success on the goal should be evaluated.
  • Time per segment: This number tells how long completing a single segment takes for a single character.
  • A number of successes: This is the threshold of success, at which the goal is generally considered to have a positive turnout. The Characters working on this Progress Clocks must succeed at least this many times to win this Progress Clocks.
  • A number of failures: This is the threshold of failure, at which the goal is generally considered to have a negative turnout. The Characters working on this Progress Clocks must not reach this number of failed actions on the goal to avoid the negative consequences.

    The number of successes and number of failures sum up to the number of segments plus 1.

Creating a Progress Clock

A typical Progress Clock could look like this:
Decypher the encoded letter: 8*1h - 6S/3F
This Progress Clock defines the overall goal to be the decoding of a script. The Clock has 8 segments and the timeframe for each segment is 1 hour, meaning this task will take 8 hours in total to complete. The number of successes is 6 and the number of failures is 3, this means that the characters must achieve at least 6 successes before they reach 3 failures in order to succeed at decyphering this letter.

Advancing the goal

For a Character to advance an existing Progress Clock, the character must perform an Action whose outcome aligns with the Clocks goal. When the Character does that, they roll a normal Skill Check to see whether they advance the goal or not:

  • On a simple success or failure, the result is entered into the clock and the clock advances by one segment.
  • Critical result variants double the number of successes/failures written on the clock and advance the clock by double the amount.

Supporting another character

It is possible for a Character to help out another Character that is currently working on a Progress Clock by rolling a Link Approach with the substituting Skill being the one used by the Character directly working on the Progress Clock. Should the help consist of less direct actions, for example gathering appropriate materials, an appropriate Skill is to be used instead.

Finishing the task

Once the worked on Clock has been filled out completely, the task is completed. How well the Characters did on the task and whether the goal has been accomplished depends on the numbers of successes and failures the Characters have added to the Clock:

  • If the number of required successes has been reached before the number of failures was, the Characters are successful in their task.
  • If the number of maximum failures has been reached before the number of successes was, the Characters fail in their task.
  • If the number of required successes has been surpassed byat least half of the number of failures (rounded down), the Characters succeed critically.
  • If the number of maximum failures has been surpassed by at least half of the number of successes (rounded down), the Characters fail critically.
Succeeding a Progress Clock

The party is tasked with decyphering an encrypted letter. The task can be worked on in 1-hour segments and will take about 8 hours of work. The smartest two Characters share the workload and help each other out to advance the goal faster. After just 4 hours of their combined effort they are able to share their results with the rest of the party. The segments of the Progress Clock are filled in as follows: S-S-S-F-S-S-S-S. This sums up to 1 failure and 7 successes. As they have reached the required number of successes, they do succeed at the task and are able to read the decyphered letter. Even better yet, because they surpassed the number of successes by half of the number of failures rounded down (3/2 = 1.5 -> 1), they succeed critically, thus are able to extract more info out of the letter than expected.

Failing a Progress Clock

The party is tasked with decyphering an encrypted letter. The task can be worked on in 1-hour segments and will take about 8 hours of work. The smartest two Characters share the workload and help each other out to advance the goal faster. After just 4 hours of their combined effort they are able to share their results with the rest of the party. The segments of the Progress Clock are filled in as follows: F-F-S-S-F-F-S-F. This sums up to 3 successes and 5 failures. As they have reached the maximum number of failures, they fail at the task and are not able to read the decyphered letter. Even worse yet, because they surpassed the number of failures by half of the number of successes rounded down (6/2 = 3), they fail critically. The party not only does not get the correct letter content, they manage to somehow misinterpret the whole letter and assume different information based on the wrong results.

Weapons, Armor and Equipment

Equipment

During skirmishes, carrying weapons and wearing armor has oftentimes proven effective to amplify ones chances of success for reaching their goal, be it defeating other characters, invading a base, or simply surviving a battle. In 5D, weapons and armor are pieces of equipment that a character has in their inventory.

Weapons

Weapons are the main way of increasing both the reach of an attack damage output of said attack. To track weapons effectively and efficiently, they all fall into this scheme:

  • Name: every weapon has an identifier. By default it is a descriptor of the weapon, eg "Boxing Gloxes", but players are free to rename weapons however they like, as long as it is clear what weapon they are talking about.
  • Type (Skill): every weapon that is used to attack relies on some form of wielder skill. For melee weapons, this is usually Might, ranged weapons usually use Agility. Advanced weapons that don't require manual input typically require Wits, and magical weapons might rely on Charms.
  • Reach: the effective range the weapon is meant to be used in. If the target is too close, the attack automatically stalls. If the target is too far and the weapon is a melee weapon, the attack can not hit. If the target is too far and the weapon is a ranged weapon, the attack automatically stalls. By default, there are 5 different ranges:
    Grapple, Melee, Long, Far, and Artillery.

    Miniature Fights only:
    Which range the distance between a character and the target falls under is determined by these measurement methods:
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the narrators pinky fingers width, the range is Grapple.
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the narrators index fingers and pinky fingers combined, the range is Melee.
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the distance from the narrators index pinky finger to the index finger, the range is Long.
    If the distance between the two characters is less than the narrators maximum span of a single hand, the range is Far.
    Anything outside that distance falls under Artillery.

  • Grip: most weapons need to be held in some form. Every weapon lists what kind of grip is necessary to use it properly. Improper grip can be used, if narratively justifiable, but results in a stall. By default, there are 3 different grips:
    One Handed, Two Handed and Twinned.
  • Damage Types: every attack uses some sort of damage type. Resistances and Vulnerabilities against these damage types change the injury severity on the target. By default, there are 3 different damage types:
    Blunt, Cut, Pierce
  • Extra Traits: any important additional info about the weapon gets noted down here. Example on a Twinned Grip Sable: "Twin Blades: when used with a second Theft Blade and the character has a vocation for using weapons, attacks automatically surge".

Armor

While weapons can prove useful to afflict stronger injuries on an opponent, armor helps protecting against too severe injuries.
Armor comes in three coverage tiers, each protecting more than the previous one:

  • half armor, when worn, will cover the bulk of the wearer.
  • full armor, when worn, will cover the bulk and appendages of the wearer but decrease dodging by -2
  • closed armor is made up of full armor and an ace piece, which covers the wearers weak point but decreases dodging by -4.
    Along with these three cover tiers, armor comes in different strength levels:
  • basic armor adds a static modifier of +2 for blocking.
  • refined armor adds a static modifier of +4 for blocking.
  • fortified armor adds a static modifier of +6 for blocking.

Character Creation

Character Creation

A character within 5Dice can be created within minutes of having a good idea. Using the following process you can manifest your dream easily.

1. Inspiration

It all starts with a simple idea, known as the pitch. A pitch is a narrative hook that draws the attention of the audience into the pitched idea by providing an interesting twist to an otherwise potentially mundane thought. The pitch should be no longer than two sentences.
From this pitch a concept can be formed. The concept is more concrete than the pitch in terms of how that character fits into the world, but it is not as constricting as choosing a city to be born in.
This concept is the backbone of your character design, and on a narrative level as many elements of the character creation process as possible should conform to support this core idea.

2. Becoming

After finding a concept, it is time to fill in the backstory of the character. The backstory tells about a characters past, and the reasons why they are in their current position. It is recommended to keep the backstory simple and written in bullet points for easier reference.

3. Identity

With the backstory you also know where the character stands in their own life. Now you can fill in the characters identity, which consists of their name, age, origin, and characteristics.
The character origin refers to the place they were born.
Characteristics mean special features of the character that are minor tweaks to the appearance or behavior that can be used to gain rust.

4. Goals

After the backstory it is time for the goals and aspirations. You know where the character came from, now you need to decide on where they want to go. A character can have a single goal or multiple goals. It is recommended to keep the list of goals as short as possible. Goals represent achievements that are to the character worth pursuing for their intrinsic values rather for external motivation.

5. Morals

A characters beliefs guide the character throughout the world with a filter of morale through which the character forms a certain world view, which in turn shapes their morals. From a characters origin their beliefs can be inferred, and the initial moral is copied from the kind of society they are a part of.

7. Attributes

After the narrative part of the character is created, the gameplay section can be filled in. Using the notes from the previously filled areas, decide on how to spend the characters skill points. On tier 1, a character has 2 skill points, and on tier 2 a character has 4 skill points.
All core skills of any character start at 0 skill points, which translates into a D6.
Skill points are used on skills to increase the dice value by 2 (to a maximum of 3 with a resulting D12), or a skill point may be gained by reducing one D6 to a D4.

The second part of the abilities are the professions. Every character must additionally have at least a major profession and may have a minor profession at the cost of 1 skill point. The major profession is their way of earning their daily bread, be it by playing music for others, accounting finances, robbing people on the streets or hunting animals in the wild.
The minor profession can be either a hobby, a side project that has nothing to do with the major profession, or it could even be a former job that still left some knowledge.
Professions are used during skill checks where applicable major professions grant a highroll to the result and applicable minor professions add +2 to the result.

Commerce

During their ventures, characters sometimes need some form of currency to purchase certain items from other characters. These currencies are not tracked completely, but via a commerce system.

Every character posesses a purse. In this purse, all their money is stored. A purse can hold any amount of money of any currency introduced. A purse is a physical item that can be stolen by others.
The purse consists of a list of currencies with a number ranging from 0 to 8. This number indicates the characters relative wealth in that currency. Item prices are measured in this relative value as well.

Commerce Levels

The relative values for characters are:

  • 1: Pauper
  • 2: Commoner, Paycheck-to-Paycheck-Liver
  • 3: Tradesperson- Craftsperson
  • 4: Accomplished Career, Small Business Owner
  • 5: Wealthy, Successful Merchant, Minor Nobility
  • 6: CEO, Nobility
  • 7: Magnate, Tycoon, High Nobility
  • 8: King / Queen, Head of Megacorporation
Important

It is important to note that these levels of commerce increase in wealth exponentially instead of linearly.

Tip

The commerce level of a character is only representing the amount of currency that player has on them, it does not include personal assets or wares to be sold.

Examples for relative item values are:

  • 0: A basic meal, worn down clothes, a beer, a magazine
  • 1: Basic food for a day, a night at an inn, a new item of clothing, a bottle of high value alcoholics
  • 2: An extravagant night out, a basic weapon, common livestock, full camping gear, simple transport
  • 3: A well-crafted weapon, a decent horse/car/vehicle, simple armor, a small living space
  • 4: A very fancy horse/car/vehicle, a nice home, an extravagant weapon, full plate armor, hired bodyguard
  • 5: A mansion, luxury transport, estate, a personal guard or security forces, established business
  • 6: A castle/skyscraper, enterprise level company, large army, a town, a significant military asset
  • 7: A metropole, armada, small country
  • 8: Kingdom, Nation
Important

Likewise with character commerce levels, item values increase exponentially instead of linearly.

Example

James is a well-off buseness man. He inherited a small fortune from his grandparents. In sum, this elevates his commerce level to tier 5. Should James acquire enough money to buy a new sportscar, this would only affect his commerce after a couple times over, due to exponential increases in wealth status.

Trading

When a character decides to engage in trading, this usually results in either a bargain, where both parties agree on swapping assets with no money involved, or a trade.
A trade is defined by one or more characters purchasing one or more items of some value from one or more other characters.

The commerce levels of the buyer and seller are affected as follows:

  • A character buying an item of a value higher than their own commerce level is not possible, they can not afford it.
  • A character buying an item of a value equal to their own commerce level reduces their own commerce level by 2 tiers.
  • A character buying an item of a value one tier less than their own commerce level reduces their own commerce level by 1 tier.
  • A character buying an item of a value at least two tiers less than their own commerce level does not reduce their own commerce level.
  • A character selling an item of a value higher than their own commerce level increases their own commerce level to that item values commerce level.
  • A character selling an item of a value queal to their own commerce level increases their commerce level by 1 tier.
  • A character selling an item of a value less than their own commerce level does not increase their own commerce level.
Example

James has wants to acquire the new sportscar he layed eyes on for a while now. His current commerce level of 5 matches the sportscars commerce value. On purchase James loses 2 commerce levels, bumping his fluid monetary value down to tier 3. This commerce level will take a while to recover to its normal level.

Example

James has has enough of his new sportscar. He wants to sell it. As he is currently commerce level 3, selling the sportscar will move it up to 5 again.

Haggling

When trying to make a deal with another character, a character can always try to increase their profits by haggling. This is resolved as three chained conflicts, with each character rolling a choice of INT or CHA each time. The character that rolled the higher result more gets to shift the items commerce value by one tier in a direction of their choosing.

Example

James tries to haggle during the sell of his sportscar. He rolls his charisma, which gives him a D10. His trade partner rolls intelligence, which gives them a D8. James rolls a 3, a 6 and a 5. His partner rolls an 8, a 2 and a 3. James has won the haggle 2 times, while his partner only won 1 time. Thus, James is allowed to move the item value by one tier to get himself a higher profit. He chooses to push the price up in order to get to a higher commerce level. Unfortunately, the trader is unable to afford this new item value and thus is unable to complete the trade.

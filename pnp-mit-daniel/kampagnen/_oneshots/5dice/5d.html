Preamble

0. Preamble

Mortal Engines is set in a post-apocalyptic world, where humanity has rebuilt its cities to traverse the unforgiving lands. No connecting roads between cities allow motorized vehicles to find their way around the outskirts, instead most intercity travel happens on lighter-than-air floating airships.
The game of Mortal Engines: XXXXXX is about resourcefulness, clever solutioms to problems, and preplanning. This game throws the players into the world, with the game master acting as their five senses. They each embody one or more characters to roam the lands of the hunting grounds, the static settlements of the Anti-Traction-League, or any other place on earth, to trade goods with witting salespeople, to uncover dark secrets within conspiracies in the fight for survival between the cities.
The game masters role is to provide an interactive world for the players that can be changed as they desire, they will take note of the players actions and let the world react accordingly. The players role is to be curious about the world and story the game master is presenting, and to interact with elements of the world in creative ways to find solutions to challenges the game master prepares for the player characters to overcome.

Skill Checks

Skill Checks
Skill Checks

Skill Checks are a tool to determine the outcomes of an unforseen interaction. They can be used whenever a character attempts something and the result is not fully predetermined.

Setting up the difficulty

In order for a character to see how well they do on a given skill check, the narrator has to set the difficulty. This is a numeric value ranging from 0 to 10 that increases linearly with rising difficulty of the action.
When a player announces that their character tries to do something with a specific intention where the outcome is not predetermined, the narrator decides how difficult this action would be for an average human. Should the character in question not be of human lineage, the difficulty gets adjusted with a relative static modifier (as per the lineage info).

Example

Barbara wants to pick a lock. Because the lock is quite old, the narrator sets this skill check as a difficulty 4 RFX check. Barbara must now succeed with at least a 4 on this check.

Example

Grimsby is trying to jump up a tall ledge. The narrator sets the skill check as a difficulty 6 STR check. As he is a kangaroo and not a human, he gets to reduce the difficulty by 2 for jumping related actions. Thus, Grimsby has to reach at least a 4 in STR to succeed in this action.

Performing Skill Checks

To perform a skill check, a character must roll the dice resulting from a characters skill level in the skill to be rolled on:

Skill Points Dice
-1 (Inept) D4
0 (Novice) D6
1 (Journeyman) D8
2 (Expert) D10
3 (Master) D12
Example

Barbara tries to pick the lock. Her RFX skill is at +1, giving her access to a D8.

Example

Grimsby has 2 skill points in STR, allowing him to roll a D10 on his attempt to jump up the ledge.

Result Modifications

There are two ways a result of a skill check can be changed: momentum rolls and static modifiers.

Momentum Rolls
Momentum Rolling means rolling the same skill check, and taking the higher or lower result, depending on whether you are surging (higher result) or stalling (lower result).
A character performs a surge once at least one active effect grants them surge, but no stalling effect is applied.
Likewise, a character performs a stall once at least one active effect grants them stall, but no surging effect is applied.
Should a character have any combination of at least one surging and at least one stalling effect applied, they cancel each other out.

Static Modifiers
Static Modifiers are a way to adjust the result of a roll (including a momentum result) and adding a small number to it, be it positive or negative.
After the dice roll is made, the player adds all applicable static modifier to the result, forming a new result.

The result of a skill check gets modified first by any applicable momentum (if a character is affected by both a surge and a stall, they cancel each other out) and then applying any static modifiers to the roll.

Example

Barbara tries to pick an old lock using her lockpicking equipment. This picklock grants her a simple +1 on lockpicking actions. Barbara rolls an 8. The picklocks static modifier boosts her result to a 9.

Example

Grimsby is attempting to jump up a ledge. Before jumping off, he takes a running start. The narrator rules that this gives Grimsby a surging momentum. His first roll is a 2, which is not enough to succeed. His second roll results in a 4. Not much better, but enough to reach the difficulty.

Interpreting the result

Comparing the result to the difficulty allows for interpretation of the outcome:

Result - Difficulty Outcome
< -4 Critical Fail
-4 to -1 Simple Fail
0 Complicated Success
1 to 4 Simple Success
> 4 Critical Success
Example

Barbara rolled a 9 when trying to lockpick an old lock. The difficulty was set to 4. The comparison between the result and the difficulty determines a +5, resulting in a simple success. Barbara is able to pick the lock easily.

Example

Grimsby achieved a 4 when trying to jump up the ledge. As a kangaroo, he was able to reduce the difficulty for him to a 4. This barely reaches the difficulty, earning him a complication. The narrator describes how Grimsby takes a running start, jumps off and is able to easily reach the ledges height, but he jumped too early and now hangs on the edge of the ledge with his paws. The hard jolt on his arms strains the muscles and bones, and Grimsby has received a small injury through this action.

The Character Sheet

Character Sheet
Character Sheet

Characters in 5D consist of a multitude of facettes. All of them fall into two categories, which are the gameplay properties and the narrative properties.

Narrative

On a narrative level, each character has an identity and a characterization of this person. The identity and chacterization form the backbone of how a character acts and views the world around them in any given situation.

Identity

Property Explanation
Name How the character gets referred to by others
Age How long this character has been roaming earth
Origin Where that character has been born
Characteristics Mechanically not significant details to the character, ie changes in looks, behavioral ticks, etc

The identity of a character consists of statistical data to describe measurable information about this character.
This consists of the name and age of the character, as well as their origin and characteristics.
The origin is a characters home place. It tells where the character was born, what kind of life they likely have lived, and pushes a probable belief system onto the character, which is based on the society they are a part of.
The characteristics are a list of detail facts about a character. They can range from noticeable appearance changes, to behavioral ticks. These characteristics do not have any mechanical impact on the gameplay, they serve as roleplay hooks to make the character more interesting.

Characterization

Facette Related question
Backstory How did the character come to be who and where they are now?
Goals What is the biggest dream the character wishes to achieve?
Beliefs What does the character believe in? Do they follow a strict moral code of some higher authority?

The characterization section consists of a characters background story, their goals and aspirations, and their beliefs and morals.
The background story aims to explain briefly, why and how that character got to the point they are in at the beginning of the story. It is recommended to keep this section as short and concise as possible.
The goals and aspirations inform what a character wants to accomplish in their life. This can be a long term goal like "seeing the entire world", or a number of smaller milestones like "trade with different nationalities salesmen" and "buy my own airship".
The beliefs and morals of a character give guidelines for how a character views the world and how they interact with it. This is influenced a lot by the origin society, as they typically lay a ground work for what a character gets served as facts, and what they are allowed to accept as truth.

Gameplay

On a gameplay level a character has differently scored abilities. These abilities are separated into core skills and professions.
Likewise, a character holds different currencies in their purse, holds, wears and carries unique equipment in their hands, holsters and pockets, and has suffered different injuries, each with distinct effects and (dis-)abilities.

Abilities

Abilities lie at the core of everything the character can do. They inform about the strengths and weaknesses of the character, and what areas of expertise they can excel at.

Core Skills

Every character has access to four core skills. These are:

  • Strength (STR):
    STR is useful for being able to sustain more bodily harm, displaying athletic talent, or hitting things harder.
  • Reflex (RFX):
    RFX gets used for dodging attacks, piloting a machine, or performing acrobatic feats.
  • Intelligence (INT):
    INT is the characters wits. This includes their ability to deduce, being more able to craft objects together, or holding a lot of knowledge in their memory.
  • Charisma (CHA):
    CHA displays the ability to trick and persuade other characters, and to read a characters true intentions.

Professions

A character does something with the time they are given. What they decide on doing results in their profession. Professions act as bonuses to use when appropriate for skill checks. A character can have two professions: a major profession, this is their main activity for earning their livelihood, and a minor profession, this can be a side gig, a former job they are still somewhat knowledgeable in, or a simple hobby they like to tend to in their free time.
A character must have at least a major profession, but they are not required to fill in the minor profession.

Status

The character status consists of four main elements: the characters purse, which is used four tracking different currencies of the character, their inventory, which holds space for different objects they carry with them, tables for injuries and Stress they have endured, and a metacurrency known as rust.

Purse

A characters purse is the home of any coin they can acquire. It is a list of all currencies and their amount a character owns. A currently is useful in one or more specific regions to buy certain goods within those regions. Some regions do not accept money from other regions. A currency always has a name and a number, that number being the amount of that currency the character carries around with them.

Inventory

The inventory of a character consists of 3 main areas:

  • Hands:
    The hands hold active objects. They can be used immediately and without losing extra time or experiencing other drawbacks like having to drop another item. Every character has two hands by default, which can be used to carry two handed objects.
  • Pockets:
    Pockets are available through a characters clothing. Pockets can hold small objects like old tech devices or knifes, but nothing larger than the size of one pocket. To be used, objects stored in a pocket have to be taken out of that pocket and put into an empty hand as an action. Every character has 4 pockets by default, which can not be used to store larger objects.
  • Body:
    The body area can be used to attach extra containers to a character, like a holster to hold a gun, or a backpack to carry more and larger items. The body area can also be used to wear armor. Objects in some containers need 2 actions to be taken out, one for unequipping the container to make it accessible, and one for taking out the item.

Injuries & Stress

Characters can receive two types of damage.
Physical damage is represented by injuries, ranging from strain to wounds to traumas to lethalities. Mental damage is represented by stress, ranging from agitation to panic to burnout to meltdowns.
Any damage is entered into the table with the data points of its name, the severity of the damage, the hit area, and whether the damage has been treated temporarily.
The injury name informs about the type of damage taken. The severity dictates the minimum row size to be reserved for that damage. The hit area and the treatment checkbox mark disabled limbs and indicate any to be treated damages in order to access the noted limb again.
In addition to the injuries table, a character also has a small simplified portrait of their body on the character sheet. This portrait is used both as indication area to mark specific areas like limbs or the head as injured, and as a place to draw a characters appearance onto the sheet if so desired. To not interfere these two uses, a checkbox next to each hit area is used as marker for whether that area is damaged or not.

Rust

Rust is a metacurrency reflecting the chaotic nature of the world at any interaction.
A character can collect an amount of rust equal to their tier.
It is gained by a character performing badly (a failed taks on a D10 or better grants 1 rust). Once per scene, a character may also gain 1 Rust by performing an in-character action that is amusing, distracting, or absurd, as long as it does not directly help the current situation.

Rust can be spent in these circumstances:

  • before a skill check to grant yourself advantage or a high roll (can be taken twice for both effects)
  • outside of a turn based encounter to retroactively modify the scene in order to gain a slight narrative advantage
  • outside of a turn based encounter to make the game master perform a corrosion roll
Corrosion Rolls

When a Corrosion Roll is triggered, the GM rolls 1d20 to represent the world’s reaction—unexpected, ironic, or dramatically useful.

This isn't a simple pass/fail—it's narrative entropy at work. The rusted seams of the world give way in strange and story-rich ways.

  • Low rolls (1–6): Compounding failure, danger, or new complication.
  • Mid rolls (7–14): Complicated success, ironic outcomes, or narrative shifts.
  • High rolls (15–20): Unintended success, surprise boons, or poetic reversals.

Failing to climb a tree? It falls over, crushing the fence you wanted to vault.
Fumbling a disguise? The enemy mistakes you for someone even more important.

Character Creation

Character Creation
Character Creation

A character within Mortal Engines: XXXXXX can be created within minutes of having a good idea. Using the following process you can manifest your dream easily.

1. Inspiration

It all starts with a simple idea, known as the pitch. A pitch is a narrative hook that draws the attention of the audience into the pitched idea by providing an interesting twist to an otherwise potentially mundane thought. The pitch should be no longer than two lines of text and is written down as the very first thing of your character.
Secondly, from this pitch can a concept be formed. The concept is more concrete than the pitch in terms of how that character fits into the world, but it is not as constricting as choosing a city to be born in.
This concept is the backbone of your character design, and on a narrative level as many elements of the character creation process should conform to support this core idea.

2. Backstory

After finding a concept, it is time to fill in the backstory of the character. The backstory tells about a characters past, and the reasons why they are in their current position. It is recommended to keep the backstory simple and written in bullet points for easier reference.

3. Identity

With the backstory you also know where the character stands in their own life. Now you can fill in the characters identity, which consists of their name, age, origin, and characteristics.
The character origin refers to the place they were born.
Characteristics mean special features of the character that are minor tweaks to the appearance or behavior that can be used to gain rust.

4. Goals & Aspirations

After the backstory it is time for the goals and aspirations. You know where the character came from, now you need to decide on where they want to go. A character can have a single goal or multiple goals. It is recommended to keep the list of goals as short as possible. Goals represent achievements that are to the character worth pursuing for their intrinsic values rather for external motivation.

5. Beliefs & Morals

A characters beliefs guide the character throughout the world with a filter of morale through which the character forms a certain world view, which in turn shapes their morals. From a characters origin their beliefs can be inferred, and the initial moral is copied from the kind of society they are a part of.

6. Abilities

After the narrative part of the character is created, the gameplay section can be filled in. Using the notes from the previously filled areas, decide on how to spend the characters skill points. On tier 1, a character has 2 skill points, and on tier 2 a character has 4 skill points.
All core skills of any character start at 0 skill points, which translates into a D6.
Skill points are used on skills to increase the dice value by 2 (to a maximum of 3 with a resulting D12), or a skill point may be gained by reducing one D6 to a D4.

The second part of the abilities are the professions. Every character must additionally have at least a major profession and may have a minor profession at the cost of 1 skill point. The major profession is their way of earning their daily bread, be it by playing music for others, accounting finances, robbing people on the streets or hunting animals in the wild.
The minor profession can be either a hobby, a side project that has nothing to do with the major profession, or it could even be a former job that still left some knowledge.
Professions are used during skill checks where applicable major professions grant a highroll to the result and applicable minor professions add +2 to the result.

7. Starting Equipment

A characters starting equipment heavily depends on a multitude of factors, like the origin, the characters upbringing, their backstory, the professions, or their morals. It is best practice to work with the game master on this point, as the starting equipment can vary wildly between characters.

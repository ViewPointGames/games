Character Sheet

Characters in 5D consist of a multitude of facettes. All of them fall into two categories, which are the gameplay properties and the narrative properties.

Narrative

On a narrative level, each character has an identity and a characterization of this person. The identity and chacterization form the backbone of how a character acts and views the world around them in any given situation.

Identity

Property Explanation
Name How the character gets referred to by others
Age How long this character has been roaming earth
Origin Where that character has been born
Characteristics Mechanically not significant details to the character, ie changes in looks, behavioral ticks, etc

The identity of a character consists of statistical data to describe measurable information about this character.
This consists of the name and age of the character, as well as their origin and characteristics.
The origin is a characters home place. It tells where the character was born, what kind of life they likely have lived, and pushes a probable belief system onto the character, which is based on the society they are a part of.
The characteristics are a list of detail facts about a character. They can range from noticeable appearance changes, to behavioral ticks. These characteristics do not have any mechanical impact on the gameplay, they serve as roleplay hooks to make the character more interesting.

Characterization

Facette Related question
Backstory How did the character come to be who and where they are now?
Goals What is the biggest dream the character wishes to achieve?
Beliefs What does the character believe in? Do they follow a strict moral code of some higher authority?

The characterization section consists of a characters background story, their goals and aspirations, and their beliefs and morals.
The background story aims to explain briefly, why and how that character got to the point they are in at the beginning of the story. It is recommended to keep this section as short and concise as possible.
The goals and aspirations inform what a character wants to accomplish in their life. This can be a long term goal like "seeing the entire world", or a number of smaller milestones like "trade with different nationalities salesmen" and "buy my own airship".
The beliefs and morals of a character give guidelines for how a character views the world and how they interact with it. This is influenced a lot by the origin society, as they typically lay a ground work for what a character gets served as facts, and what they are allowed to accept as truth.

Gameplay

On a gameplay level a character has differently scored abilities. These abilities are separated into core skills and professions.
Likewise, a character holds different currencies in their purse, holds, wears and carries unique equipment in their hands, holsters and pockets, and has suffered different injuries, each with distinct effects and (dis-)abilities.

Abilities

Abilities lie at the core of everything the character can do. They inform about the strengths and weaknesses of the character, and what areas of expertise they can excel at.

Core Skills

Every character has access to four core skills. These are:

  • Strength (STR):
    STR is useful for being able to sustain more bodily harm, displaying athletic talent, or hitting things harder.
  • Reflex (RFX):
    RFX gets used for dodging attacks, piloting a machine, or performing acrobatic feats.
  • Intelligence (INT):
    INT is the characters wits. This includes their ability to deduce, being more able to craft objects together, or holding a lot of knowledge in their memory.
  • Charisma (CHA):
    CHA displays the ability to trick and persuade other characters, and to read a characters true intentions.

Professions

A character does something with the time they are given. What they decide on doing results in their profession. Professions act as bonuses to use when appropriate for skill checks. A character can have two professions: a major profession, this is their main activity for earning their livelihood, and a minor profession, this can be a side gig, a former job they are still somewhat knowledgeable in, or a simple hobby they like to tend to in their free time.
A character must have at least a major profession, but they are not required to fill in the minor profession.

Status

The character status consists of four main elements: the characters purse, which is used four tracking different currencies of the character, their inventory, which holds space for different objects they carry with them, tables for injuries and Stress they have endured, and a metacurrency known as rust.

Purse

A characters purse is the home of any coin they can acquire. It is a list of all currencies and their amount a character owns. A currently is useful in one or more specific regions to buy certain goods within those regions. Some regions do not accept money from other regions. A currency always has a name and a number, that number being the amount of that currency the character carries around with them.

Inventory

The inventory of a character consists of 3 main areas:

  • Hands:
    The hands hold active objects. They can be used immediately and without losing extra time or experiencing other drawbacks like having to drop another item. Every character has two hands by default, which can be used to carry two handed objects.
  • Pockets:
    Pockets are available through a characters clothing. Pockets can hold small objects like old tech devices or knifes, but nothing larger than the size of one pocket. To be used, objects stored in a pocket have to be taken out of that pocket and put into an empty hand as an action. Every character has 4 pockets by default, which can not be used to store larger objects.
  • Body:
    The body area can be used to attach extra containers to a character, like a holster to hold a gun, or a backpack to carry more and larger items. The body area can also be used to wear armor. Objects in some containers need 2 actions to be taken out, one for unequipping the container to make it accessible, and one for taking out the item.

Injuries & Stress

Characters can receive two types of damage.
Physical damage is represented by injuries, ranging from strain to wounds to traumas to lethalities. Mental damage is represented by stress, ranging from agitation to panic to burnout to meltdowns.
Any damage is entered into the table with the data points of its name, the severity of the damage, the hit area, and whether the damage has been treated temporarily.
The injury name informs about the type of damage taken. The severity dictates the minimum row size to be reserved for that damage. The hit area and the treatment checkbox mark disabled limbs and indicate any to be treated damages in order to access the noted limb again.
In addition to the injuries table, a character also has a small simplified portrait of their body on the character sheet. This portrait is used both as indication area to mark specific areas like limbs or the head as injured, and as a place to draw a characters appearance onto the sheet if so desired. To not interfere these two uses, a checkbox next to each hit area is used as marker for whether that area is damaged or not.

Rust

Rust is a metacurrency reflecting the chaotic nature of the world at any interaction.
A character can collect an amount of rust equal to their tier.
It is gained by a character performing badly (a failed taks on a D10 or better grants 1 rust). Once per scene, a character may also gain 1 Rust by performing an in-character action that is amusing, distracting, or absurd, as long as it does not directly help the current situation.

Rust can be spent in these circumstances:

  • before a skill check to grant yourself advantage or a high roll (can be taken twice for both effects)
  • outside of a turn based encounter to retroactively modify the scene in order to gain a slight narrative advantage
  • outside of a turn based encounter to make the game master perform a corrosion roll
Corrosion Rolls

When a Corrosion Roll is triggered, the GM rolls 1d20 to represent the world’s reaction—unexpected, ironic, or dramatically useful.

This isn't a simple pass/fail—it's narrative entropy at work. The rusted seams of the world give way in strange and story-rich ways.

  • Low rolls (1–6): Compounding failure, danger, or new complication.
  • Mid rolls (7–14): Complicated success, ironic outcomes, or narrative shifts.
  • High rolls (15–20): Unintended success, surprise boons, or poetic reversals.

Failing to climb a tree? It falls over, crushing the fence you wanted to vault.
Fumbling a disguise? The enemy mistakes you for someone even more important.
